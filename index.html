About

The 2020 IEEE MIT Undergraduate Research Technology Conference (URTC) brings together undergraduates from around the world to present, discuss, and develop solutions to advance technology for humanity. Students may publish papers of their school projects, research, innovations, or case studies. Conference attendees will attend a rich program with keynote speeches and technical talks featuring renowned speakers, a student design competition, and networking events. The 2020 URTC conference will be hosted virtually.

Guru Madhavan: Engineering Our Wicked Problems

Using narratives and case-studies, this talk will consider hierarchies of understanding and approaching problems from an engineering sense, and offer related perspectives on professional development to navigate various areas of technology, practice, and public affairs.

Guru Madhavan is director of programs of the National Academy of Engineering where he leads and oversees activities of broad scope and complexity focused on engineering practice, research, communication, ethics, and policies. His portfolio of work at the National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine has included leading the analyses for making prescription medicines affordable and developing a national strategy for cancer control in the U.S., directing a global health forum on infectious diseases, and conducting the research, design, and development of a systems analysis platform for prioritizing new vaccines and related interventions. A systems engineer by background, he received his M.S. and Ph.D. in biomedical engineering and an M.B.A. from the State University of New York. He has served as a technical adviser to the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services, has worked in the medical device industry as a research scientist developing cardiac surgical catheters for ablation therapy, and has been a strategic consultant for technology startup firms and nonprofit organizations. He has served as a vice-president of IEEE-USA and was a founding member of the Global Young Academy. Among numerous honors, he has received the National Academies’ Innovator Award, National Academy of Medicine’s Cecil Medal, AAMI–Becton Dickinson Award for Professional Achievement, Washington Academy of Sciences’ Krupsaw Award for engineering sciences and education, Professional Achievement Award from the Society of Asian Scientists and Engineers, and has been named a distinguished young scientist by the World Economic Forum. His publications include the nonfiction "Applied Minds: How Engineers Think" (W.W. Norton) that has been translated into many languages. For his books and lectures, he has received the IEEE-USA Award for Distinguished Literary Contributions Furthering Public Understanding and the Advancement of the Engineering Profession.

Sreeram (Ram) Dhurjaty: Morphing Smartphones Into Rudimentary Medical Devices for Low and Middle Income Countries

With the advent of smartphones as well as low cost platforms such as Arduinos/Raspberry Pi can enable morphing of these into rudimentary medical devices that can enable diagnosis as well as Telemedicine. A smartphone is a powerful display and computing and storage platform. Even simple smartphones have capabilities exceeding all the computers in NASA in 1969, when the first moon-landing took place. This talk will discuss morphing smartphones into rudimentary medical devices. Dr. Dhurjaty will also mention work that has been done by several contributors, in this arena.

Machine learning algorithms can enable remote diagnosis and screening of ailments, that can augment the lack of of trained medical personnel in LMICs.

Appropriate technology for LMICs has to be dealt with care. Patient safety and robustness cannot be compromised. Dr. Dhurjaty will touch on some of the standards that govern these devices that deal with safety and performance.

Dr. Sreeram (Ram) Dhurjaty, LSMIEEE, is Chair, IEEE Rochester section and a Distinguished IEEE CESoc. lecturer. He held senior positions in engineering and research, in medical systems, at Eastman Kodak Company, Analogic, and Bose Corporation before embarking on a career of full time consulting. He was educated at IIT Bombay and Yale University and has degrees in Civil Engineering, Fluid mechanics, Electronic Instrumentation, Biomedical Engineering, and Control System Theory. Over his career he has designed medical systems such as Patient Monitors, Defibrillators, CT scanners, Ultrasound Imaging Systems, Fetal Monitoring systems, Digital and Computed Radiography, Telemedicine, Telerehabilitation and precision analog circuitry for medical systems. Six of his patents, at Kodak, on retrofittable digital radiography, that replaced screen film, enabled a game changer in the field and a multi-billion dollar industry, worldwide.

He has 16 issued patents in areas of medical devices and systems. He is passionate about affordable medical systems for Low and Middle Income countries. He works closely with the Engineering World Health organization (EWH) developing medical devices for under-developed countries and is a member of the Board for the EWH organization. Ram developed a hand cranked defibrillator which was to be marketed, initially, in India. He is an expert in low noise Analog design as well high voltage magnetics. He consults in High Voltage transformer and design of X-Ray generators. Ram is a member of the AAMI standards committees on Defibrillators and ECGs. He is been a past-chair of the Boston chapter of the IEEE, EMBS, a founding member of the IEEE, SSIT and a past representative to the IEEE, Committee on Man and Radiation (COMAR). He is an emeritus member of AAPM.

Clarice Aiello: From Nanotech to Living Sensors: Unraveling the Spin Physics of Biosensing at the Nanoscale

I am a quantum engineer interested in how quantum physics informs biology at the nanoscale. An expert on high-tech sensors that work due to room-temperature quantum effects in noisy environments, I now focus on “living quantum sensors” -- organisms and cells responding to tiny stimuli, and outperforming technological probes in awe-inspiring ways. Unveiling the underlying physical mechanisms employed by “living quantum sensors” impacts: the engineering of ultrasensitive electromagnetic probes; the elucidation of natural feats such as animal navigation; and therapeutics for metabolic diseases. Can quantum physics be established -- or refuted! -- to account for physiologically relevant biosensing phenomena, and be manipulated to technological and therapeutical advantage? This is the exciting question that the Quantum Biology Tech (QuBiT) Lab at UCLA addresses.

Clarice D. Aiello was born and raised in Brazil. She trained as an experimental physicist in Europe, having earned a Diplome d’Ingenieur de l’Ecole Polytechnique in France, and an M.Phil. from the University of Cambridge, Trinity College, in England.

Research brought Clarice to the American shore, where she completed a Ph.D. in Electrical Engineering at MIT. Clarice then held postdoctoral appointments in Bioengineering at Stanford, and in Chemistry at UC Berkeley. Her work has been funded by sources as diverse as the Fulbright Commission, the Schlumberger Foundation, the Moore Foundation and UNESCO. Clarice is also a recipient of MIT’s School of Engineering’s “Graduate Student Award for Extraordinary Teaching and Mentoring”.

Clarice is now Assist. Prof. in the Electrical and Computing Engineering Dept. at UCLA, where she fearlessly leads the interdisciplinary Quantum Biology Tech (QuBiT) Lab.
